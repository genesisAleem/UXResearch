01 — Context

Why Redesign the M36 Product?

What is M36?

M36 is a digital investment platform that allows users to diversify their financial portfolio by investing in a range of financial products — mutual funds, treasury bills, and high-yield opportunities — primarily accessible as a digital wallet.

The Opportunity

We identified several opportunities for our customers — feature improvements, technical enhancements, and UI changes — for both our retail and lifestyle experience. The goal was to address real friction points discovered through user research and observation.

A financial digital product like M36 exists to remove the barriers between young Nigerians and wealth-building tools they were never taught to access.

03 — Research Insights

Usability Testing + Laws of UX

To understand decision-making while using M36, I used a combination of user observation and UX design laws to analyse where and why users got stuck.

01
Aesthetic–Usability Effect
Users often perceive aesthetically pleasing designs as easier to use. The M36 home page based on usability testing and observation shows that most users like Mrs Tijani don't find the product usable because of its aesthetics and visual design. The design actually does a good job of grouping the wallet together — but falls apart as information is chunked.
"I really don't know what to do."
— Mrs Tijani, Usability Testing Observation

Observation: Mrs Tijani does not seem to know where to go or what to do. Although familiar with traditional money tools, she doesn't seem to understand the options available. She seemed stressed out and frustrated whenever she encountered a null button, and struggled with the home page UI, investment and fund wallet.

02
Law of Similarity & Chunking
Humans often like to find simplicity and order in complex shapes — it prevents us from becoming overwhelmed with information. Colour, shape, size, orientation and association can signal that elements belong to the same group, creating a common visual experience. Grouping similar investment products together using colours or boundaries can help users like Mrs Tijani navigate better.
03
Miller's Law & Information Overload
Structure creates a clear path and effectively establishes the relationship between elements and actions. While the design does a good job of grouping the wallet together, the design begins to fall apart as information is chunked — users struggle to navigate the page without clear visual hierarchy to guide them.
"What are NGN bonds? When does my investment mature? What's my gain?"
— Mrs Okueni, Usability Testing Observation

Observation: Given the nature of the products, yield and investments involved, many investors were deeply skeptical about making decisions. In this case, not understanding the terms like "yield" or "what is a yield?" created a significant confidence barrier.

06 — Design Decisions

What Changed & Why

🎨
Colour-Based Grouping
Grouping similar investment products together using consistent colour boundaries creates visual chunks that leverage the Law of Similarity. Users no longer need to read every label — they can scan by colour association and build mental models faster.
🗂️
Structural Hierarchy
Applying Miller's Law — we reduced what's visible on first load to the highest-priority actions and grouped secondary information under expandable sections. This prevents cognitive overload on first encounter with the home page.
💬
Contextual Tooltips
Inspired by Robinhood's approach, financial terms like "yield", "maturity date", and "commercial papers" now surface in-context tooltips. Users can learn without leaving the product, building confidence incrementally.
📊
Progressive Disclosure
The BUY PAGE and COMMERCIAL PAPERS screens adopt a staged information model — showing price, gain, and key terms first, with full detail available on demand. Reduces perceived complexity dramatically.
UX Laws Applied
1
Aesthetic–Usability Effect
Users associate visual polish with functional reliability. Improving M36's aesthetics directly increased perceived trustworthiness before any interaction.
2
Law of Similarity
Shared colour, shape and size signals group membership. Used to connect related investment products visually without relying on text labels alone.
3
Miller's Law
The average person can only keep 7 (±2) items in working memory. Chunking reduces load and enables better decision-making across the home screen.
07 — Conclusion

Key Takeaways

In general, the M36 app is missing aesthetic pleasing designs. Applying UX laws and user research findings can greatly improve the perception and usability of the product. As products are diverse in nature, I believe that these products can be grouped using colours, a structural hierarchy — to help users understand what is most important.

Colour grouping — Use distinct colour territories to map investment categories, reducing cognitive load during product selection.
Structural hierarchy — Establish clear visual priority so users can identify the most important action on any screen within 3 seconds.
Financial literacy integration — Surface definitions and context inline, reducing drop-off caused by unfamiliar terminology without requiring external education.
Progressive disclosure — Present the most essential information first; let users who want depth opt into it — never overwhelm on first contact.
